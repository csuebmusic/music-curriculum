Course Information

Catalog description: Group instruction in piano for music majors. Emphasis on reading from staff notation, technique, keyboard harmony, improvisation, and repertoire.

Keyboard Musicianship II continues the three-semester sequence, extending harmonic vocabulary to inversions of primary chords, the dominant seventh, and basic accompaniment patterns such as Alberti and waltz bass. Students begin work with lead-sheet symbols and Roman numeral realization at the keyboard, harmonize in major and minor keys, and expand technique through scales and arpeggios in additional key groups. Sight-reading moves to grand staff, hands together.

Student Learning Outcomes

Upon successful completion of this course, students will be able to:

  1. 01Perform major and minor scales and arpeggios across Group 1 and Group 2 keys, two octaves.
  2. 02Play triads in all inversions and perform the dominant seventh chord in block and broken patterns.
  3. 03Harmonize short melodies using primary chords (I, IV, V⁷) in major and minor, with idiomatic accompaniment patterns including Alberti and waltz bass.
  4. 04Sing a melody while playing a two-hand accompaniment.
  5. 05Realize basic lead-sheet symbols and Roman numerals at the keyboard.
  6. 06Sight-read in grand staff, hands together, in four-to-eight-bar examples.

Learning Benchmarks

By the end of the semester, students who have successfully engaged with the course material should be able to demonstrate the following:

Keyboard II

Students can play major and minor scales and arpeggios across Group 1 and 2 keys, perform primary chords in major and minor with block and broken patterns, harmonize short melodies using diatonic chords, sing a melody while playing a two-hand accompaniment, and realize simple lead-sheet symbols. Sight-reading in grand staff hands together is established.

Making the time work

This course is classified as an activity course for a reason. Class time is not a lecture you can passively receive; it is spent at the instrument, working on technique, harmonization, sight-reading, and repertoire alongside your classmates. The hands develop through repetition, and every minute you spend at the keyboard in class matters. Show up prepared, and show up ready to play.

More importantly, you are taking three courses concurrently (Theory, Aural Skills, and Keyboard), and the combined out-of-class load across the three is substantial. Plan for it.

One schedule that works well is roughly two hours a day, Monday through Thursday, split between the three courses. Daily short sessions will outperform occasional long ones in every one of these courses. Use the practice rooms! See the Music Department facilities page for access and hours.

Primary text. This book is used across all three Keyboard Musicianship courses (MUS 118, 119, and 218). MUS 218 completes the book. Purchase it in MUS 118 and keep it for the full sequence.

Other materials: Manuscript paper and pencils with erasers for written work in class. A metronome is required for all practice sessions outside of class; a free phone app (such as Metronome Beats, Pro Metronome, or similar) is perfectly adequate and strongly recommended if you do not own a physical metronome.

Stay organized. This course distributes handouts and piano scores regularly, and the material builds directly on itself from week to week. A dedicated folder or binder for this course is essential: keep handouts and returned assignments in order so you can return to them during practice.

A few habits that matter: practice every day, even for short sessions: the hands and the ear both respond to regularity, not to cramming. When you make an error in practice, slow down rather than starting over from the beginning. Identify the specific measure that is difficult and work it in isolation before returning it to tempo. And practice with the metronome from the first day: it is much harder to correct an unsteady pulse than to build one correctly from the start.

Course Requirements

This course has four categories of graded work:

GoReact Assignments (45%): On most weeks you will prepare and video-record a few short musical items and upload them to GoReact through Canvas. These may include harmonizations, sing-and-plays, and repertoire. GoReact allows the instructor to provide time-stamped feedback directly on your recordings.

Recording and submission procedures:

  • Listen to your own recordings before uploading; submit your best take. Do not upload multiple takes; the instructor will listen only to the first one found.
  • Record one item at a time and name each file appropriately.
  • File naming convention inside GoReact: your_last_name_assignment_name.mp4.
  • Audio and video quality do not need to be perfect, but recordings should be free from excessive background noise and your hands should be visible.
  • Do not delete your submission files immediately after uploading, in case of a technical issue.

In-class Wednesday Quizzes (15%): Quizzes involve performance or written work. You will be expected to perform exercises, drills, and other materials. There may be weeks when you are not called on, but you should always be prepared. Quiz items will be announced in advance on Canvas.

Midterm Exam (20%): An individual performance exam held during Week 8. Full details will be posted on Canvas ahead of the exam.

Final Exam (20%): An individual performance exam held during Week 15. Same format as the midterm.

Evaluation criteria for playing assignments: All GoReact recordings, quizzes, and exams are assessed along the following dimensions. The rubric for quizzes is the same as for recorded assignments, though the materials are simpler.

  • Note accuracy: correct notes in the correct register and octave.
  • Fingering accuracy: fingering as given in the score or as devised in class.
  • Harmonic accuracy: melody harmonized with appropriate chords and voicings.
  • Rhythmic accuracy: correct durations for each note.
  • Tempo: performed at the requested speed.
  • Pulse: steady, even beat maintained throughout.
  • Dynamics: dynamic markings in the score followed.
  • Articulation: articulation markings in the score followed.
  • Hand position and posture: appropriate hand position and good overall posture.
  • Continuity: performed from start to finish without stopping and restarting.

Individual assignment rubric (0–4 scale): All GoReact assignments, quizzes, and exams are evaluated on a 0–4 scale.

Course Policies

Attendance. Keyboard is learned in class as well as in the practice room; they are not interchangeable. More than three unexcused absences will affect your grade. Please email the instructor in advance when possible.

Late work. Assignments submitted after the deadline will receive a 2% deduction per day late, up to a maximum deduction of 50%. This means submitting late is always better than not submitting, and work will never receive less than 50% credit solely due to lateness. The quality of the work is still evaluated normally. If you anticipate significant delays or challenges, please communicate early, in person or through Canvas.

Fingernails. Long fingernails that extend beyond the fingertip are not compatible with piano playing. You will need to keep them trimmed for the duration of the course.

Lab rules and etiquette. No food or drink in MB 2081; water bottles with lids must be placed on the floor. Wash and dry hands before using the keyboards. Do not place bags, books, or keychains on the keyboards. Handle headsets with care; always return them to their hooks when leaving. Turn keyboards off and cover the keys when you are done. When using an eraser, keep shavings away from the keys.

In-class etiquette. Play only when instructed. Never play while the instructor or a classmate is playing or speaking. If you have a technical issue (can't hear the instructor, can't hear others), raise your hand. Always check whether your keyboard is in speaker or headset mode before class begins.

Syllabus changes. Any changes will be discussed with students before implementation and communicated in person and through Canvas.

Generative AI Use Policy

Generative AI tools (such as ChatGPT, Claude, DALL·E, and similar) may be used in this course with documentation and citation. AI can be useful for brainstorming or organizing ideas, but the listening, thinking, and writing must be your own. AI cannot replace listening or reflection, and substituting AI-generated content for required analysis violates course policy.
