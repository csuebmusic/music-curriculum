Course Information

Catalog description: Development of music reading skills applicable to all instrumental and vocal performance media. Dictation, analysis and singing of melodies and melodic exercises; study and performance of basic rhythmic materials.

Aural Skills II continues where Aural Skills I ended, extending melodic vocabulary through skips involving all primary triads and the dominant seventh chord, adding sextuple compound meter and syncopation, introducing harmonic singing and harmonic listening, and beginning bass line and two-voice dictation. The course runs alongside Theory II (MUS 112) and the material of the two courses reinforces each other directly. Consistent daily practice remains essential.

Student Learning Outcomes

Upon successful completion of this course, students will be able to:

  1. 01Sight-sing melodies involving all diatonic triads in major and minor, including syncopation and sextuple compound meter.
  2. 02Take melodic, bass line, and two-voice dictation with accuracy.
  3. 03Identify harmonic rhythm, cadences, and all four triad qualities by ear.
  4. 04Perform in harmonic context through harmonic singing and basic two-voice textures.

Learning Benchmarks

By the end of the semester, students who have successfully engaged with the course material should be able to demonstrate the following:

Aural Skills II

Students can sight-sing in major and minor modes with chromatic neighbors and dominant seventh chord skips, take melodic and harmonic dictation including bass lines and two-voice textures, perform syncopation and sextuple compound meter, and identify all four triad qualities by ear. Harmonic listening through basic chord progressions is introduced and consolidated.

Making the time work

This course is classified as an activity course for a reason. Class time is not a lecture you can passively receive; it is structured around singing, conducting, dictation, and drill work done in real time with your classmates. The ear and the voice develop through repeated use, and the minutes you spend performing in class are irreplaceable. Show up prepared, and show up ready to sing.

More importantly, you are taking three courses concurrently (Theory, Aural Skills, and Keyboard), and the combined out-of-class load across the three is substantial. Plan for it.

One schedule that works well is roughly two hours a day, Monday through Thursday, split between the three courses. Daily short sessions will outperform occasional long ones in every one of these courses. Use the practice rooms! See the Music Department facilities page for access and hours.

Stay organized. Aural skills generates a lot of handouts, weekly exercises, and returned dictations. The material is cumulative: what you could not hear in week 3 will still matter in week 11. Keep a dedicated folder or binder for this course, in chronological order.

A few habits that help: review your returned dictations carefully (your errors tell you exactly what to practice next), sing through old material regularly rather than only when an exam is approaching, and treat your daily practice sessions as non-negotiable. Course Requirements

This course has five categories of graded work:

GoReact Assignments (25%): On most weeks you will prepare and video-record 2–3 short musical items and upload them to GoReact through Canvas. These may include melodies, rhythms, sing-and-plays, and keyboard tasks. GoReact allows the instructor to provide time-stamped feedback directly on your recordings.

Performance requirements for melodies:

  • Sing on solfège syllables.
  • Conduct the specified beat (quarter note, half note, etc.) while singing.
  • Perform at the specified tempo (check each item for metronome marks).

Performance requirements for rhythms:

  • Vocalize on rhythm syllables (Takadimi).
  • Conduct the specified beat while performing.
  • Perform at the specified tempo (check each item for metronome marks).

After practicing, record each item in a quiet environment. You may give yourself the tonic triad and opening note of a melody before beginning, but do not play along while you sing. A metronome in the background is encouraged and sometimes required.

Recording and submission procedures:

  • Listen to your own recordings before uploading; submit your best take. Do not upload multiple takes; the instructor will listen only to the first one found.
  • Record one item at a time and name each file appropriately.
  • File naming convention inside GoReact: your_last_name_number_in_anthology.mp4 (example: Shimron_AFSS_44.mp4).
  • Audio and video quality do not need to be perfect, but recordings should be reasonably free from background noise.
  • Do not delete your submission files immediately after uploading, in case of a technical issue.

Ear Training Assignments (25%): On most weeks you will receive an ear training assignment completed outside of class using Auralia. In-class activities will provide a foundation and strategies for the at-home tasks.

In-Class Assessments (10%): Short in-class drills, melodies, rhythms, or other performance-based tasks. These are embedded in the regular class flow rather than scheduled on a fixed grid, and they happen throughout the semester without advance notice, which means the only reliable preparation is consistent daily work. Some weeks most of the room is assessed, other weeks only a few students are called on, and the assessments act as a snapshot of how you are handling the skills being developed while helping identify areas that need more work.

Midterm Exam (20%): A performance-based exam held during Week 8, combining GoReact video submissions and ear training. Full details will be posted on Canvas ahead of the exam.

Final Exam (20%): A performance-based exam held during finals week (Week 16), with the same structure as the midterm. Full details will be posted on Canvas ahead of the exam.

Individual assignment rubric (0–4 scale): All GoReact and Ear Training assignments are evaluated on a 0–4 scale. The same scale applies to exam components.

ScoreLevelDescription
4OutstandingRare. The finest representation of the work: accurate pitch and rhythm, steady pulse, correct solfège or rhythm syllables, coordinated conducting, musical continuity, and confident independent delivery.
3Very goodClear mastery of the material in precise form. Above average work with minimal errors.
2Below averageThe material has been learned but needs more thought and preparation. Not yet at a passing level of execution.
1UnacceptableDemonstrates less than the required effort and only minimal understanding of the material.
0Not submittedNo submission, unlearned music, or work that does not engage with the assignment.

Grading criteria for melodies: accurate pitch; accurate durations; steady pulse; appropriate solfège syllables; coordinated conducting at the assigned tempo; continuity (not stopping); independence (not relying on an instrument to navigate the melody); attention to musical details including expression, dynamics, articulation, and tempo; overall confidence in delivery.

Grading criteria for rhythms: accurate durations; steady pulse; appropriate rhythm syllables (Takadimi); coordinated conducting at the assigned tempo; continuity; independence; attention to musical details including expression; overall confidence in delivery.

Course Policies

Attendance. Aural skills cannot be made up outside of class. More than three unexcused absences will affect your grade. Please email the instructor in advance when possible.

Late work. Assignments submitted after the deadline will receive a 2% deduction per day late, up to a maximum deduction of 50%. This means submitting late is always better than not submitting, and work will never receive less than 50% credit solely due to lateness. The quality of the work is still evaluated normally. If you anticipate significant delays or challenges, please communicate early, in person or through Canvas.

Syllabus changes. Any changes will be discussed with students before implementation and communicated in person and through Canvas.

Generative AI Use Policy

Generative AI tools (such as ChatGPT, Claude, DALL·E, and similar) may be used in this course with documentation and citation. AI can be useful for brainstorming or organizing ideas, but the listening, thinking, and writing must be your own. AI cannot replace listening or reflection, and substituting AI-generated content for required analysis violates course policy.
