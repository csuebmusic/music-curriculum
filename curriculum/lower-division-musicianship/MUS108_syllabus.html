Course Information

Catalog description: Introduction to the materials and structures of Western tonal music, including notation, rhythm, scales, intervals, chords, and form. Students practice listening, analysis, and short creative projects while exploring how music communicates expression, cultural meaning, and identity across settings.

This course assumes no prior musical training. It is designed for students curious about how music works and what it means, regardless of background. By the end of the semester, you will be able to read and write basic musical notation, identify the building blocks of tonal music by ear and by sight, and speak about music with greater precision and confidence.

No auditions, performances, or prior instrument experience are required. Active listening, curiosity, and consistent engagement with the weekly material are the most important things you can bring.

Student Learning Outcomes

Upon successful completion of this course, students will be able to:

  1. 01Apply music theory terminology to read, write, and describe musical notation (pitches, rhythms, intervals, scales, chords, cadences, and forms).
  2. 02Demonstrate how fundamental musical concepts support expression and communication in music.
  3. 03Evaluate their own aesthetic responses to music using theoretical vocabulary.
  4. 04Examine how cultural and historical contexts shape the use and perception of musical fundamentals.
  5. 05Describe how diverse identities influence the creation, performance, and reception of music.
  6. 06Identify the roles that music plays in different settings (worship, entertainment, protest, community).

Learning Benchmarks

By the end of the semester, students who have successfully engaged with the course material should be able to demonstrate the following:

Reading & Rhythm

Students can read pitches in treble and bass clef, interpret note values and rests in simple and compound meters, and correctly identify and write ties, dotted rhythms, syncopation, triplets, and duplets.

Scales & Keys

Students can construct major scales and all three forms of minor scales, identify key signatures through the full circle of fifths, and identify and construct intervals by number and quality. Roman numeral analysis and form are not covered in this course.

Harmony

Students can construct and identify major, minor, diminished, and augmented triads in root position. Triad inversions, seventh chords, Roman numerals, and formal analysis are introduced in subsequent courses.

Cultural & Historical Context

Students can discuss how musical fundamentals function within specific cultural and historical settings, drawing on examples from Western classical, popular, and global musical traditions, and can articulate how their own aesthetic responses connect to what they are hearing.

Music in Diverse Settings

Students can identify the roles music plays across different contexts (worship, entertainment, protest, community) and describe how diverse identities shape the creation, performance, and reception of music.

Out-of-class time in this course is primarily spent on listening, written exercises, and journal work. The listening component in particular rewards repeated engagement: pieces that seem opaque on first hearing often open up considerably by the third or fourth listen.

Making the time work

For many of you, this is your first semester of college, and you are figuring out the shape of university life at the same time you are taking this course: new campus, new expectations, new rhythms of eating, sleeping, studying, and being alone or with people. That is a real adjustment, and it is worth naming honestly. You do not need to have it all figured out in week one, and you do not need to be a natural musician to do well here. What you do need is a regular, protected pocket of time for this course every week.

One pattern that works well: an hour, three or four days a week, rather than one long push before class. Music rewards repetition. A short daily listening session, even twenty or thirty minutes, builds the ear more effectively than a two-hour session the night before an assignment is due. The same is true of the written work: doing one exercise each day feels lighter and produces better results than saving them all for Sunday night.

Bring questions to class. Bring your listening notes, your written work in progress, and anything that confused you in the reading. Use office hours early in the semester rather than only when something has gone wrong. The students who thrive in MUS 108 are not the ones who arrive already knowing music theory, they are the ones who show up consistently and engage.

Course Requirements

This course has five categories of graded work:

Lesson Exercises (30%): Weekly written assignments drawn from the Takesue workbook and supplementary Karpinski exercises, graded for completion and effort. These exercises reinforce the week's concepts and help you apply the technical language of music while building notation fluency.

Quizzes (20%): Short in-class group quizzes testing comprehension of key concepts: notation, pitch, rhythm, scales, chords, cadences, and form. Quizzes also connect musical structures to their cultural and historical contexts. Group quizzes cannot be made up if missed without a documented excuse.

Listening Journals (20%): Three reflections (2–3 pages each) combining technical analysis with aesthetic, cultural, and personal reflection, due in Weeks 5, 9, and 13 by Sunday evening. Each journal has two parts:

Part 1: Listening Response: Choose one or two pieces from the course listening examples and write about them using the theory vocabulary we are studying at that time. Describe what you hear, explain how the musical structure affects your experience, and connect it to cultural or historical context.

Part 2: Personal Playlist Reflection: Choose one additional piece from any genre or tradition that connects to the journal topic. Explain why you chose it, how it relates to the musical concept, and what role it plays in your life or community.

Final Creative Project (15%): A short creative project applying the musical fundamentals covered in class. Students will compose or arrange an original piece of their choosing and submit a written reflection (1–2 pages) connecting their musical choices to the concepts, cultural contexts, and personal experiences explored throughout the semester. The project is submitted in stages across the second half of the semester: a proposal in Week 10, a draft in Week 12, and the final version with a brief in-class presentation in Week 15. Full guidelines will be provided on Canvas. No prior performance experience is required; submissions may include notation, a lead sheet, a chord chart, or an audio recording.

Final Exam (15%): a cumulative exam held during the final class meeting (Week 15).

Course Policies

Attendance. Regular attendance is essential. Music is learned through doing: listening, group work, and in-class practice cannot be replicated outside of the classroom. If you need to miss a class, please email the instructor in advance when possible. More than three unexcused absences will affect your grade.

Late work. Assignments submitted after the deadline will receive a 2% deduction per day late, up to a maximum deduction of 50%. This means submitting late is always better than not submitting, and work will never receive less than 50% credit solely due to lateness. The quality of the work is still evaluated normally. If you anticipate significant delays or challenges, please communicate early, in person or through Canvas.

Syllabus changes. Unanticipated circumstances may require adjustments to this syllabus. Any changes will be discussed with students before implementation and communicated in person and through Canvas.

Generative AI Use Policy

Generative AI tools (such as ChatGPT, Claude, DALL·E, and similar) may be used in this course with documentation and citation. AI can be useful for brainstorming or organizing ideas, but the listening, thinking, and writing must be your own. AI cannot replace listening or reflection, and substituting AI-generated content for required analysis violates course policy.
