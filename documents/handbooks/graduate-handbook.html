welcome from the graduate coordinator

Welcome to graduate study in music at Cal State East Bay. The M.A. is a focused, self-directed degree: you arrive already fluent on your instrument or in your art, and the two years ahead are about deepening that fluency, widening the questions you ask of music, and finishing a body of work that is unmistakably yours.

You will move through a shared seminar core that spans analysis and post-tonal practice, the history and theory of jazz, interdisciplinary collaboration, conducting, the social and ecological dimensions of sound, entrepreneurship, and teaching at the college level. Alongside that core you will study privately in your area of emphasis, perform in an ensemble each semester, and build toward two culminating milestones: a public capstone performance and a comprehensive examination. This Handbook lays out how those pieces fit together and what each one asks of you.

Treat your Graduate Coordinator as your first point of contact for anything about the program: sequencing, advancement to candidacy, the capstone, the exam, or simply thinking through the shape of your degree. I am glad you are here, and I look forward to the music you will make.

Inés Thiebaut, Graduate Coordinator, CSUEB Department of Music

02the m.a. in music

overview

The Master of Arts in Music is a 32-unit graduate degree completed across four semesters of full-time study. It pairs a shared graduate seminar core with private study and ensemble work in a chosen area of emphasis, and it culminates in two requirements: a public capstone performance and a comprehensive examination.

The seminar core is common to every emphasis and spans analysis and post-tonal practice, the history and theory of jazz, interdisciplinary collaboration, advanced conducting, the social and ecological dimensions of music, entrepreneurship in the arts, and teaching music in higher education. The emphasis study, four semesters of applied lessons and four semesters of ensemble, is where students concentrate in performance, conducting, or composition. The capstone (MUS 693) is taken across the final three semesters and prepares the public culminating performance.

areas of emphasis

Students declare one area of emphasis at admission:

  • Performance (Classical or Jazz): concentrated applied study and ensemble work on the student's instrument or voice, in either the classical or the jazz track, building to a public capstone recital.
  • Choral Conducting: advanced conducting study and podium work with the Department's choral ensembles, building to a capstone conducting performance.
  • Composition: the creation of a body of original acoustic and electroacoustic work, building to a capstone concert of the student's music.

03graduate coursework

graduate seminars

Eight graduate seminars form the shared academic core of the M.A., common to all three emphases and accounting for 17 of the program's 32 units. They run across the four semesters alongside applied study and ensemble; the roadmap in section 02 gives the standard sequence. Seminars are offered on a rotation, so take them as scheduled in consultation with the Graduate Coordinator.

  • MUS 601Analysis of Musical Styles: Birth and Death of TonalityTraces the evolution of tonality from the Renaissance through the Romantic era, developing musicological research method and analytical skill through comparative analysis of selected works.2 units
  • MUS 602Analysis of Musical Styles: Post-Tonality and BeyondA research and analysis seminar on music from the turn of the century to World War II, working across the period's divergent styles within an evolving theoretical framework.2 units
  • MUS 603Entrepreneurship in the ArtsEntrepreneurship in the arts and music industry: power and social identity in arts careers, the business and logistics of the field, personal branding, and a marketing plan built toward a chosen path.3 units
  • MUS 604Sounding Justice: Unveiling the Ecological and Social Dimensions of MusicThe intersection of music, environment, and social justice, approached through critical analysis, ethical reflection, and applied projects that put music to work for change.2 units
  • MUS 605History and Theory of JazzAn in-depth study of jazz history and theory: key movements, influential figures, harmonic and improvisational practice, and the cultural forces that shaped the genre.2 units
  • MUS 606Interdisciplinary Collaboration in MusicComposers, conductors, and performers collaborate on projects that fuse music with other arts, adapting traditional practice for multidimensional, interdisciplinary performance.2 units
  • MUS 630Advanced ConductingEnsemble conducting technique covering gesture, score analysis, and ear training across genres and periods, with advanced rehearsal technique and program building.2 units
  • MUS 631Teaching Music in Higher EducationPreparation for post-secondary teaching: learning theory, curriculum development, assessment, and materials for teaching music at the college level.2 units
conducting placement

A student who comes to Advanced Conducting (MUS 630) without prior conducting study may be invited to take one or both of the upper-division undergraduate conducting courses first, MUS 341 (basic conducting) and MUS 441 (intermediate conducting), depending on their level.

04applied study and ensembles

The performance core of the degree pairs applied study with ensemble in every semester. Graduate applied lessons (MUS 661–668) carry 2 units and a 60-minute weekly lesson, and one unit of ensemble runs alongside, across all four semesters. Every student begins applied study at Level 6a and advances through a jury before the area faculty at the end of each semester. Registration for applied lessons requires concurrent enrollment in an ensemble, per Music Department policy.

applied lesson policies

The Graduate Coordinator registers each student for applied lessons automatically, from the graduate plan and the area of emphasis declared at admission. The student then arranges the lesson day and time by mutual consent with the instructor. Lessons are 2 units, a 60-minute weekly lesson, held on the CSUEB campus throughout the semester.

absence policy

Instructors are not obligated to reschedule lessons a student misses, except when the absence is for a required university activity. If an instructor misses a lesson, it is rescheduled. A student who misses a lesson for a university activity (an ensemble tour, for instance) and notifies the instructor in advance is given a makeup, and coordinating the makeup is the student's responsibility. Lessons that fall on regular school holidays will be rescheduled. A student more than ten minutes late may be counted absent, and more than one unexcused absence in a semester may lower the applied grade.

applied leave of absence

A student who returns to applied study after an absence of more than one semester may be required to audition to confirm current level placement.

secondary applied area

A student may add a second applied area after completing one semester in the primary area. A second area requires a placement audition, which can place the student anywhere across levels 1 through 4 or at level 6. A placement at levels 1 through 4 carries 1 unit of applied study; a placement at level 6 carries 2 units. The primary applied instructor is consulted to confirm that a second area is compatible with progress in the first, and requests are subject to studio space and department finances. Direct the request to the Graduate Coordinator.

change of applied instructor or area

Applied study at this level depends on an extended relationship with one teacher, so changing studios is discouraged. When a change is necessary, the process mirrors the undergraduate one.

change of applied instructor

  1. The student raises the difficulty with the current instructor, preferably in person. This conversation often resolves it. If it does not:
  2. The student submits a written account to the coordinator of the applied area, detailing the difficulty and proposing solutions. The coordinator discusses it with the instructor.
  3. A meeting is scheduled among the student, the coordinator, and the instructor to work through the difficulty and possible solutions.
  4. The student studies with the current instructor for one more full semester to attempt a resolution. The coordinator or the instructor may waive this semester if either judges the situation irreparable.
  5. If, after juries, the student has stopped progressing (as judged by the coordinator) or the difficulty is irreparable, the coordinator reassigns the student to a new studio. The student may request, but is not guaranteed, a specific teacher.

change of area of emphasis

Changing the area of emphasis is a formal step at the graduate level. The student resubmits the emphasis-specific materials required for admission to the new area (the audition recording, conducting video, or portfolio of scores and audio, as the area requires) and must be accepted into the new emphasis before the change takes effect. The new area's requirements for units, juries, and benchmarks then apply.

coaching

Vocal Coaching (MUS 660A) and Instrumental Coaching (MUS 660B) are 2-unit courses in which a student works with a faculty coach on repertoire that calls for a collaborative pianist. Coaching is distinct from applied lessons: it carries its own syllabus, expectations, and attendance, and does not count as applied lesson units. Eligibility depends on instructor workload, applied progress, and department finances. A student is free to arrange a collaborative pianist independently and is not required to enroll in coaching to secure one for the capstone.

individual practice expectations

The Department recommends a baseline of 3 to 4 hours of practice a day, five days a week, with more as the work and the area demand; performance students in particular should expect to practice well beyond that baseline. Practice room availability and procedures are in section 01 and on the Music Department website. Composition students are expected to give equivalent time to composing, producing, and working in the electronic music studios; contact the Studio Director (Inés Thiebaut, MB 2012) to arrange studio access.

ensembles

One unit of ensemble runs with applied study every semester (MUS 351–355, 360–369), four units in total. Offerings and meeting times vary by semester; current information is on the ensembles page. Auditions and placement take place at the first meeting of each semester: register for the course and attend the first class. The ensembles below are grouped by the area they primarily serve, and the groupings overlap.

The home for choral conducting students and for classical and jazz voice.

05applied juries and Level 6

The undergraduate ladder ends at Level 4, the senior bar. Graduate applied study begins at Level 6: the program reserves Level 5 for the experience a musician gains between the degrees, the performing, teaching, and living that mature an artist before graduate study, a level in its own right though it carries no jury.

Every graduate applied student therefore works at Level 6. Four juries run across the four semesters, each before the area faculty at the end of the term: 6a, 6b, and 6c are progress juries, and 6d is the capstone recital jury.

Level 6a is the technique-benchmark semester, and the catch-up valve. It confirms that the student commands the full technical roster carried up from the undergraduate ladder, extended here to a graduate standard: across the full practical range of the instrument, faster and cleaner, with chromatic scales and the area-specific work listed below. A student who arrives in full command demonstrates it and moves on; a student who arrives short uses 6a to reach the bar before the repertoire load builds. The area faculty may add to a student's technical checklist in consultation with the student.

6b and 6c carry the 6a technique forward, which the jury may spot-check, and advance the repertoire toward the capstone. The capstone program is proposed and approved at 6c, so the student enters the final semester with a locked program. 6d is the capstone: a one-hour recital, approximately fifty minutes of music, clearly above the senior recital, performed to a professional standard and submitted with the capstone paper.

the capstone paper

The capstone recital is submitted with a written paper of roughly eight to twelve pages, plus program notes. It is a solid paper on the capstone, not a thesis. It sets out the reasoning behind the program, how it was built and sequenced and why; a deep dive into the inspirations and influences behind it; and the issues and ideas the program engages. Program notes for each work follow, with form, context, and proper citation. The paper draws on the research and analysis the graduate seminars train (MUS 601, 602, and 604) and is submitted at the 6d jury.

requirements by area

The eight areas follow the undergraduate jury areas. Each gives the 6a technique benchmark, the repertoire at each progress jury, and the 6d capstone program. Tempos, octave counts, and added patterns are set by the area faculty with the student.

classical woodwinds and brass

level 6a progress jury · technique benchmark

technique benchmark

  • The full scale-and-arpeggio roster (all major; all minor in natural, melodic, and harmonic forms; all augmented and diminished), across the full practical range of the instrument, slurred and tongued, at a brisk tempo.
  • Chromatic scales over the full range.

repertoire

Two to three substantial works by different composers above the senior level, contrasting in style, not previously juried. Fifteen minutes minimum.

level 6b progress jury

Two to three substantial works by different composers, not previously juried, broadening the style periods and building toward the capstone program. The 6a technique is carried forward and may be spot-checked. Fifteen minutes minimum.

level 6c progress jury · capstone program approved

Capstone-level repertoire drawn from the proposed recital program, which is presented for area-faculty approval at this jury. Technique carried forward. Twenty minutes minimum.

level 6d capstone recital jury
  • A one-hour recital, approximately fifty minutes of music, above the senior level.
  • All style periods represented (Baroque, Classical, Romantic, and Contemporary, 20th–21st century) across a variety of composers.
  • At least two multi-movement works.
  • At least one chamber work with other performers.
  • At least three works from memory.
  • Accompanied by a pianist where the repertoire calls for it.
  • Submitted with the capstone paper.

classical percussion

level 6a progress jury · technique benchmark

technique benchmark

  • Mallets: the full scale-and-arpeggio roster across the full range, four-mallet, at a brisk tempo, plus chromatic scales.
  • Snare: advanced rudiments and rolls at performance tempo.
  • Timpani: tuning, intervals, and roll control across the drums.
  • Orchestral excerpts: timpani, mallets, and snare; the jury asks for two.

repertoire

Three contrasting works above the senior level, including a four-mallet solo from memory, not previously juried. Fifteen minutes minimum.

level 6b progress jury

Three contrasting works, broadening styles and building toward the capstone program. The 6a technique and excerpts are carried forward and may be spot-checked. Fifteen minutes minimum.

level 6c progress jury · capstone program approved

Capstone-level repertoire drawn from the proposed program, which is presented for area-faculty approval at this jury. Technique carried forward. Twenty minutes minimum.

level 6d capstone recital jury
  • A one-hour recital, approximately fifty minutes of music, above the senior level.
  • Include a multiple percussion solo, a four-mallet marimba or vibraphone solo from memory, and a timpani solo.
  • At least two multi-movement works.
  • At least one chamber work with other performers.
  • A variety of composers, eras, and styles.
  • Submitted with the capstone paper.

classical strings and guitar

level 6a progress jury · technique benchmark

technique benchmark

  • The full scale-and-arpeggio roster in three octaves (chromatic scales in two), connected without stops, at a brisk tempo. String players add two-octave double-stop scales; guitarists cover the full fingerboard with the modes and diatonic arpeggios.
  • Two etudes or technique pieces, prepared from memory.

repertoire

Two to three substantial works by different composers above the senior level, not previously juried. Fifteen minutes minimum.

level 6b progress jury

Two to three substantial works by different composers, not previously juried, broadening the style periods toward the capstone program. The 6a technique and etudes are carried forward and may be spot-checked. Fifteen minutes minimum.

level 6c progress jury · capstone program approved

Capstone-level repertoire drawn from the proposed program, which is presented for area-faculty approval at this jury. Technique carried forward. Twenty minutes minimum.

level 6d capstone recital jury
  • A one-hour recital, approximately fifty minutes of music, above the senior level.
  • All style periods represented across a variety of composers.
  • At least two multi-movement works.
  • At least one chamber work with other performers.
  • At least three works from memory.
  • String players are accompanied by a pianist where the repertoire calls for it, at most one unaccompanied piece unless the applied instructor approves more. The guitar's solo repertoire is performed unaccompanied.
  • Submitted with the capstone paper.

classical voice

level 6a progress jury · technique benchmark

technique benchmark

  • A prepared vocalise or technical study demonstrating agility, range, and breath control.
  • Accurate diction in Italian, German, French, and English.

repertoire

Four selections, not previously juried, in at least three languages, including one aria.

level 6b progress jury

Four to five selections, not previously juried, broadening toward all style periods and four languages, including an aria. The 6a technique is carried forward. Building toward the capstone program.

level 6c progress jury · capstone program approved

Selections drawn from the proposed recital program, which is presented for area-faculty approval at this jury.

level 6d capstone recital jury
  • A one-hour recital, approximately fifty minutes of music, above the senior level.
  • At least four languages represented.
  • All style periods represented.
  • At least two arias, one of them advanced.
  • A song cycle or set performed complete.
  • Submitted with the capstone paper.

classical piano

level 6a progress jury · technique benchmark

technique benchmark

  • All major and minor scales and arpeggios, four octaves, in parallel and contrary motion, at a brisk tempo.
  • Scales in thirds, sixths, and octaves.
  • Chromatic scales.

repertoire

  • Three selections by three different composers above the senior level, about twenty minutes total, at least two memorized.
  • Selections may not repeat from the previous jury; individual movements are permissible.
level 6b progress jury

Three selections by three different composers, broadening the style periods toward the capstone program, at least two memorized, no repeats from the previous jury. Technique carried forward.

level 6c progress jury · capstone program approved

Capstone-level repertoire drawn from the proposed program, which is presented for area-faculty approval at this jury. Technique carried forward.

level 6d capstone recital jury
  • A one-hour recital, approximately fifty minutes of music, above the senior level, all memorized, with exceptions for complex 20th- and 21st-century repertoire.
  • Selections from at least four style periods.
  • A Baroque work: a Well-Tempered Clavier prelude and fugue, or a complete suite.
  • A complete Classical sonata, all movements.
  • A substantial Romantic or 20th–21st-century work.
  • Submitted with the capstone paper.

jazz melodic instruments

The saxophones, trumpets, trombones, and the other wind, brass, and string instruments played in jazz. Jazz keeps its own scale language, complete by this level. A transcribed solo and tunes played melody-and-improvisation make up the repertoire, building to the capstone.

level 6a progress jury · technique benchmark

technique benchmark

  • The complete jazz scale ladder in all twelve keys, at a brisk tempo and applied over the changes: the Level 1 modes and blues, the bebop scales, the melodic-minor and harmonic-minor modes (including the altered scale and Phrygian dominant), and the symmetric scales (diminished half-whole and whole-half, and whole-tone).

repertoire

  • A transcribed solo, performed with the recording.
  • At least three tunes above the senior level, not previously juried: play the melody and improvise over the form.
level 6b progress jury

At least four tunes, not previously juried, harder and broadening the book toward the capstone program, with a transcribed solo. The scale ladder is carried forward and may be spot-checked.

level 6c progress jury · capstone program approved

Tunes drawn from the proposed capstone program, which is presented for area-faculty approval at this jury.

level 6d capstone recital jury
  • A one-hour recital, approximately fifty minutes of music, all memorized, each tune arranged with at least an intro, an outro, and an ending, above the senior level.
  • A boppish tune with a difficult melody (for example, Donna Lee, Anthropology, or Conception).
  • A John Coltrane composition (Giant Steps, 26-2, or Countdown).
  • Two or more standards above the senior level, one of them a Latin tune.
  • A standard arranged in an odd meter.
  • An original tune.
  • A tune of the student's choice.
  • Submitted with the capstone paper.

jazz rhythm section

Guitar, bass, and piano. Rhythm-section players prepare the same scale ladder and tunes as the melodic instruments, and in addition comp the changes (guitar and piano) or walk a chorus (bass) on the jury's tunes.

level 6a progress jury · technique benchmark

technique benchmark

  • Comp the changes (guitar and piano) or walk a chorus (bass) on the jury's tunes.

repertoire

  • A transcribed solo, performed with the recording.
  • At least three tunes above the senior level, not previously juried: play the melody and improvise over the form.
level 6b progress jury

At least four tunes, not previously juried, harder and broadening the book toward the capstone program, with a transcribed solo and comping or walking throughout. The scale ladder is carried forward.

level 6c progress jury · capstone program approved

Tunes drawn from the proposed capstone program, which is presented for area-faculty approval at this jury.

level 6d capstone recital jury
  • A one-hour recital, approximately fifty minutes of music, all memorized, each tune arranged with at least an intro, an outro, and an ending, above the senior level.
  • A boppish tune with a difficult melody (for example, Donna Lee, Anthropology, or Conception).
  • A John Coltrane composition (Giant Steps, 26-2, or Countdown).
  • Two or more standards above the senior level, one of them a Latin tune.
  • A standard arranged in an odd meter.
  • An original tune.
  • A tune of the student's choice.
  • Comp the changes (guitar and piano) or walk a chorus (bass) throughout the program.
  • Submitted with the capstone paper.

jazz drums

The drum set. In place of the instrumental scale requirement, drummers prepare a keyboard requirement. On the kit they play the head, solo over the form, trade fours, and cover the required styles and tempos.

level 6a progress jury · technique benchmark

technique benchmark

  • Keyboard: on piano, all major and minor scales, ii–V–I voicings in several keys, and a jazz tune played with a walking bass line or comping.
  • Kit: advanced styles and tempos; play the head on the kit, solo over the form, and trade fours.

repertoire

  • A transcribed solo, performed with the recording.
  • At least three tunes above the senior level, not previously juried: head on the kit and solo over the form, trading fours, across contrasting styles and tempos.
level 6b progress jury

At least four tunes, not previously juried, harder and broadening the book toward the capstone program, with a transcribed solo. The keyboard requirement is carried forward.

level 6c progress jury · capstone program approved

Tunes drawn from the proposed capstone program, which is presented for area-faculty approval at this jury.

level 6d capstone recital jury
  • A one-hour recital, approximately fifty minutes of music, all memorized, each tune arranged with at least an intro, an outro, and an ending, above the senior level.
  • A bebop tune with a difficult melody: play the melody on the kit and trade fours with the band.
  • A John Coltrane tune (Giant Steps, 26-2, or Countdown): solo over the form.
  • Two or more standards above the senior level, with at least one very up-tempo tune and one Latin tune, leading the band in with a solo intro, in or out of time, without a count-off.
  • A standard arranged in an odd meter.
  • An original tune.
  • A tune of the student's choice.
  • Submitted with the capstone paper.
