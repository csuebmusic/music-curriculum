welcome from the graduate coordinator

Welcome to graduate study in music at Cal State East Bay. The M.A. is a focused, self-directed degree: you arrive already fluent on your instrument or in your art, and the two years ahead are about deepening that fluency, widening the questions you ask of music, and finishing a body of work that is unmistakably yours.

You will move through a shared seminar core that spans analysis and post-tonal practice, the history and theory of jazz, interdisciplinary collaboration, conducting, the social and ecological dimensions of sound, entrepreneurship, and teaching at the college level. Alongside that core you will study privately in your area of emphasis, perform in an ensemble each semester, and build toward two culminating milestones: a public capstone performance and a comprehensive examination. This Handbook lays out how those pieces fit together and what each one asks of you.

Treat your Graduate Coordinator as your first point of contact for anything about the program: sequencing, advancement to candidacy, the capstone, the exam, or simply thinking through the shape of your degree. I am glad you are here, and I look forward to the music you will make.

Inés Thiebaut, Graduate Coordinator, CSUEB Department of Music

preface

The requirements for the Master of Arts in Music are stated in the University Catalog. This Handbook supplements two University authorities and does not replace either: the University Catalog, which states degree requirements, and the University Graduate Student Handbook, published by the Office of Graduate Studies, which governs University-wide graduate policy such as advancement to candidacy, residency and currency of work, academic standing, and the culminating-experience framework. Where this Handbook and the University Graduate Student Handbook differ, the University's policy governs, and you should bring the discrepancy to the Graduate Coordinator so it can be corrected.

01general information

advising

Graduate students are advised by the Graduate Coordinator, who is the first point of contact for program planning, course sequencing, advancement to candidacy, and the two culminating requirements (the capstone performance and the comprehensive examination). Meet with the Graduate Coordinator before registering each semester, and at any point when a question about the degree comes up.

Day-to-day administrative questions (registration mechanics, forms, room and key access) are handled by the music office. University-level processes that sit outside the Department (admission, advancement to candidacy, graduation clearance) run through the Office of Graduate Studies, described below. BayAdvisor shows your degree progress and lets you schedule appointments.

02the m.a. in music

overview

The Master of Arts in Music is a 32-unit graduate degree completed across four semesters of full-time study. It pairs a shared graduate seminar core with private study and ensemble work in a chosen area of emphasis, and it culminates in two requirements: a public capstone performance and a comprehensive examination.

The seminar core is common to every emphasis and spans analysis and post-tonal practice, the history and theory of jazz, interdisciplinary collaboration, advanced conducting, the social and ecological dimensions of music, entrepreneurship in the arts, and teaching music in higher education. The emphasis study, four semesters of applied lessons and four semesters of ensemble, is where students concentrate in performance, conducting, or composition. The capstone (MUS 693) is taken across the final three semesters and prepares the public culminating performance.

areas of emphasis

Students declare one area of emphasis at admission:

  • Performance (Classical or Jazz): concentrated applied study and ensemble work on the student's instrument or voice, in either the classical or the jazz track, building to a public capstone recital.
  • Choral Conducting: advanced conducting study and podium work with the Department's choral ensembles, building to a capstone conducting performance.
  • Composition: the creation of a body of original acoustic and electroacoustic work, building to a capstone concert of the student's music.

03admission & placement

admission requirements

Applicants must meet both the University admission requirements and the Music Department's requirements to be admitted as a classified graduate student. The Department requires a bachelor's degree in Music from an accredited institution and good academic standing at the last college or university attended. Request official final transcripts from every institution attended, before and after the bachelor's degree, sent to the Office of Admissions.

Apply through CSUApply. As part of the Music Department application, submit:

  • Two letters of recommendation from teachers or colleagues who can assess your readiness for graduate study.
  • A statement of purpose, two pages, describing your objectives for graduate study in music and your short- and long-term goals.
  • Evidence of your ability in the area of emphasis you wish to pursue, as described below.

evidence by emphasis

  • Classical Performance: a video recording or link of at least two representative works from your bachelor's senior recital or comparable repertory, with three or four pieces drawn from multiple style periods expected. Pianists submit representative works from the senior recital or comparable repertory.
  • Jazz Performance: a recording demonstrating proficiency on your instrument or voice in representative jazz repertory.
  • Choral Conducting: a video recording or link of at least fifteen minutes of a rehearsal with a vocal ensemble or choir, optionally with a concert performance video. Final candidates are invited to campus to audition.
  • Composition: scores (PDF) and recordings (MP3) of three to four representative compositions for varied combinations of instruments or voices. For electroacoustic or tape music, include a PDF information page listing the titles, the software used, and a brief description of the compositional process.

applied placement

Applied-area placement may take place any time up to the start of the Fall or Spring semester; scholarship audition materials count as applied-area placements for applicants who complete them. Placement sets your starting applied level and, together with current offerings, your ensemble assignment. Registration for applied lessons requires concurrent enrollment in an ensemble, as a matter of Department policy.

international transcripts

California residents and U.S. citizens with international transcripts are classified as international students for admission evaluation and are processed by the International Admissions Office, though they pay resident fees if they meet California residency.
