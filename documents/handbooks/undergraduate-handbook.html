welcome from the chair

Welcome to the world of advanced music study. While this path may offer challenges alongside its joys, the rewards are unparalleled. Music is not only entertainment. It is a transformative force capable of shaping one's understanding of life, love, and the full spectrum of human experience, both a privilege and a responsibility.

Though some of you envision careers in music education, composition, production, or performance, others may be drawn to different landscapes. Regardless of your ultimate destination, the skills honed during your musical journey will serve you well.

Consider the qualities valued by employers: critical thinking, collaboration, professionalism, communication, and leadership. Music education is a rigorous training ground for each of these attributes and more. You will dissect complex musical structures, solve interpretive dilemmas, and master the art of expressing yourself through a universal language. Ensembles and solo practice cultivate teamwork, discipline, and self-reliance. Through performances, you will hone your communication skills, both verbal and nonverbal, and learn to connect with audiences on a profound level. Leadership opportunities abound, whether through student boards or the power dynamics inherent in collaborative music-making.

The Department of Music faculty and staff extend a warm welcome to you as you embark on this enriching path. We are proud to present you with a finely crafted program accredited by the National Association of Schools of Music, a sturdy framework upon which your musical identity will take shape. Embrace the challenges, the joys, the rigorous practice sessions, and the exhilarating performances. Work diligently, and remember that we are here to guide you every step of the way.

Buddy James, Professor and Chair, CSUEB Department of Music

b.a. in music requirements

The B.A. in Music requires a total of 120 units: 54 units of music major requirements, 52 units of General Education and Graduation Requirements, and up to 14 units of free electives. The degree serves students with career goals in performance, composition, music technology, jazz, teaching, and graduate study. CSUEB is an accredited institutional member of the National Association of Schools of Music (NASM).

Program roadmaps for both 4-year (freshman entry) and 2-year (ADT transfer) pathways are available at the B.A. program page. Students should consult their music advisor to create an education plan tailored to their needs.

admission and applied area placement

While there are no additional admission requirements beyond university admissions, all music majors must pass an applied area placement to begin taking lessons in their primary applied area. Once in lessons, students follow the applied jury schedule. Students are encouraged to plan their placement carefully to avoid delays in graduation.

keyboard musicianship requirement

All CSUEB Music students must complete three semesters of group keyboard study (at a prior institution or at CSUEB), or complete all three levels of the Keyboard Musicianship credit-by-exam, in order to graduate. The most efficient path is to complete the Keyboard Musicianship sequence: MUS 118, MUS 119, and MUS 218.

Working on proficiency requirements independently is highly discouraged and may significantly delay graduation. Students who wish to demonstrate mastery by exam may only do so during the first two weeks of the semester while registered for the relevant Keyboard Musicianship course.

recital attendance requirement

Music majors are required to attend twelve (12) music recitals each semester they are enrolled as a music major. Attendance at any official Music Department event counts as one (1) credit. Participation or performance in a Music Department event counts as one-half (½) credit.

applied music study

general information

Music majors must declare a principal performance medium, departmentally approved by audition or the acceptance of a Creative Portfolio, in one of the following areas: Composition / Music Production, Flute, Oboe, Clarinet, Saxophone, Bassoon, Trumpet, French Horn, Trombone, Euphonium, Tuba, Percussion, Violin, Viola, Cello, Bass, Guitar, Piano, Voice, or Conducting.

Jazz lessons are an option for upper-division undergraduate students through audition, after the student has successfully demonstrated solid technique and the fundamentals of jazz improvisation, such as through completion of the jazz improvisation courses.

applied area auditions

All music majors in the B.A. in Music program require a successful applied area placement in order to start taking lessons in their primary area of study. Students only do this once. Once in lessons, students follow the applied jury schedule depending on their entry level.

You don't need to complete your applied area placement right away when you start the B.A. in Music (very common for first-year students, who can build up musicianship before taking lessons), but if you want to graduate in two (2) years (most transfer students), you should expect to start lessons right away.

Applied area placements can take place at any time, up until the start of the Fall or Spring semester. Scholarship audition materials will be considered applied area placements for those applicants who complete them.

classical performance

Piano: Two or three works of contrasting styles, at least one of which must be memorized. Pieces should be chosen from the standard Classical repertoire and last no more than ten minutes in total. 20th- and 21st-century works are welcome. Individual movements of multi-movement works (such as sonatas or suites) are acceptable.

Voice: Two contrasting selections totaling no more than ten minutes. At least one song should be in a language other than English. Classical songs or arias, folk songs, and musical theater selections are preferred. Selections should be memorized and sung with accompaniment.

Percussion: Two contrasting works totaling no more than ten minutes (sections of works are acceptable in order to stay within the ten-minute timeframe). Show a contrast of instruments (for example, snare drum and marimba).

All other instruments: Two contrasting works totaling no more than ten minutes (sections of works are acceptable in order to stay within the ten-minute timeframe).

jazz performance

Drums: Demonstrate the following styles: medium-tempo swing, up-tempo 50s bebop, medium-tempo jazz waltz, bossa nova (medium), Afro-Cuban (medium to fast), funk, and hip-hop (any tempo). Bluetooth access will be provided for backing tracks.

All other instruments: Two contrasting works totaling no more than ten minutes (sections of works are acceptable in order to stay within the ten-minute timeframe). You will also be required to demonstrate improvisation skills. Bluetooth access will be provided for backing tracks.

music composition

Two original compositions (PDF scores and audio) that best represent your musical potential and creativity. They can be for any genre and instrumentation. We are looking for both scores and recordings (a MIDI export from your notation program is acceptable).

music production

Two original songs (audio only) that best represent your musical potential and creativity. Any style is welcome.

enrollment requirements

residency requirement

Undergraduate students must complete a minimum of 4 units of applied music study in residence at CSUEB to obtain a B.A. in Music. All 9 units of the applied music study and performance block (lessons, major ensemble, and chamber ensemble) must be completed in residence.

concurrency requirement

Registration in applied lessons requires concurrent enrollment in both a major performing ensemble (MUS 351–359) and a minimum of five (5) total music units for the semester. Students who drop below this threshold mid-semester should consult their advisor immediately, as it may affect their applied lesson enrollment and progress.

individual practice expectations

The Department recommends a minimum of 1½ to 2 hours of practice per day, 5 days per week, as a baseline for adequate degree progress. Some students may need more depending on skill level and background. Practice room availability and reservation procedures are listed on the Music Department website and in section 01 of this handbook.

Composition and Music Production students are expected to dedicate equivalent time to composing, producing, and working in the electronic music studios. Regular studio time in MB 2508 and MB 1506 is available to composition and production majors. Contact the Studio Director (Inés Thiebaut, MB 2012) to arrange access.

applied lesson policies

Students who have successfully passed an audition or submitted a Creative Portfolio may request applied study through the Department of Music prior to the end of the first week of the semester. The day and time of lessons are arranged by mutual consent between instructor and student. Students should contact their applied instructor to arrange lesson times no later than the end of the first week.

  • Students should register for 1 unit of applied study each semester they wish to take lessons.
  • Lessons are 30 minutes per unit and should occur weekly throughout the semester.
  • All lessons should take place on the CSUEB campus.
  • A faculty member may recommend that a student excelling in applied study receive 2 units (60 minutes per week). Requests must come no later than finals week of the prior semester. The Department Chair makes final decisions after reviewing transcripts, degree progress, and department finances. 2-unit requests must be renewed each semester and may be discontinued at any point.
  • No student may be enrolled in more than 2 units of applied lessons in any given semester.

absence policy

Instructors are not obligated to reschedule lessons missed by students, except when the absence is due to a required university activity. If an instructor misses a lesson, it will be rescheduled. If a student misses a lesson due to a university activity (such as an ensemble tour) and notifies the instructor in advance, a makeup lesson will be provided. It is the student's responsibility to coordinate the scheduling of makeup lessons.

Instructors are not obligated to reschedule lessons that fall on regular school holidays. If a student is more than ten minutes late, the student may be considered absent. More than one unexcused absence per semester may result in a lowered applied music grade. Instructors will notify students and the Music Office of any instructor absences as soon as possible, and makeup lessons will be scheduled promptly.

applied leave of absence

A student who returns to applied music instruction after an absence of more than one (1) semester may be required to audition to determine an accurate current level placement.

applied lessons for non-majors

Music Minors and non-music majors who perform in a Major Performing Ensemble may be eligible for applied music study. Requests in writing (email) should come from Major Performing Ensemble leaders to the Department Chair by the end of the second week of the semester. The Department Chair, in consultation with the Applied Music Committee, will evaluate each request according to available studio space and department finances. Final decisions will be made by the second week of the semester.

change of instructor or applied area

change of applied instructor within the same applied area (studio)

Studying applied music at the university level demands a great deal of time and refinement, and this level of refinement can only occur through extended study with a single teacher. Changing applied studios is therefore discouraged. The ideal undergraduate education places a student with one teacher for their entire period of study, to benefit from a long-term relationship and the development of a common musical and technical language. Should difficulties arise where a student wishes to change applied studios, the process is as follows.

  1. The student should communicate their difficulties to their current instructor, preferably in person. Often this conversation will resolve perceived problems. If difficulties remain after this initial conversation:
  2. The student will submit an official document to the Coordinator of their applied area detailing the difficulties and outlining proposed solutions. The Coordinator will discuss this document with the private instructor.
  3. A meeting will be scheduled between the student, the Coordinator, and the private instructor to discuss the difficulties and potential solutions.
  4. The student will study with the current instructor for one more complete semester to attempt to resolve the difficulties. The Coordinator or the private instructor may waive this semester if either believes the situation is irreparable.
  5. Following juries, if the student has stopped progressing (as determined by the Coordinator) or if the difficulties have become irreparable, the Coordinator will reassign the student to a new applied studio. The student may request, but is not guaranteed, a specific teacher. All applied instructors are quality professionals in their field, and placements are made based on the needs of the student and the department.
change of applied area

Students who wish to change their primary applied area must successfully pass a formal audition or creative portfolio review in the new area of study, as outlined on the Music Department website and the audition portion of this handbook. Students who successfully change their primary applied area must adhere to department policy regarding required units, benchmarks, juries, and other requirements.

secondary applied areas

Music majors may request instruction in a second applied area. Requests are considered on a semester-by-semester basis. To qualify:

  • The student must have earned an A in their primary area of study for the previous two semesters.
  • The student should submit a formal request by email to the Department Chair explaining why they wish to study a secondary area and describing their background in it.
  • Students who are beginners in the requested secondary area will be directed to first take the relevant Basic Instrument class.
  • The student's primary applied instructor will be consulted to confirm that study in a secondary area will not hinder progress in the primary area.
  • The Department Chair, in consultation with the Applied Music Committee, will evaluate each request according to student academic progress, ensemble participation, available studio space, department finances, and other factors.
  • No student may be enrolled in more than 2 units of applied study during any semester.
  • Final decisions will be made by the second week of the semester.

coaching

Instrumental and Vocal Coaching (MUS 281/481, MUS 282/482) are courses where a student enrolled in applied study works with a faculty coach on repertoire requiring a collaborative pianist. Coaching is not the same as applied lessons: it does not count as applied lesson units and carries its own syllabus, expectations, and attendance requirements. Coaching units do not count toward graduation requirements. Students are not required to enroll in coaching to secure an accompanist for a recital, and are free to find accompanists independently.

eligibility and priority

Eligibility is determined by available instructor workload, applied academic progress, and departmental finances. When demand exceeds availability, the following priority system is in place: senior recitalists, then junior recitalists, then noon recitalists, then ad hoc recitalists.

units

  • Senior and Junior Recital performers should enroll in two (2) units the semester of the recital.
  • All other students should enroll in one (1) unit per semester.

applied juries and levels

Applied study at CSUEB is organized around a progression of levels, with jury and performance requirements at each stage. This section describes how levels work, what juries are required, and how students are assessed. Current procedures and area-specific requirements are also available at the juries page.

  • Each student taking applied study is assigned a level ranging from 1a to 4b, corresponding to year and semester of study (1a = first year, first semester; 2b = second year, second semester, and so on).
  • All undergraduate students begin at Level 1a and register for MUS 2xx lessons; once they reach Level 3a, they register for MUS 4xx lessons. Students who study a secondary applied area begin at Level 1a in that area.
  • Applied requirements are fulfilled after the successful completion of 4 units of applied study, at any level, so some students complete only Levels 1 and 2. Advanced study through Level 4b is recommended for students interested in graduate study or music education. Any units above the 4 required are converted into music electives, of which students need 12 total.
  • Transfer students with applied units from another institution who possess upper-division skills may opt to perform an Advancement Jury to Level 3a at the conclusion of any semester, with the agreement of their applied instructor.
two-year transfer path

Transfer students who wish to complete both a Junior and Senior Recital while graduating in two years should perform an Advancement Jury to Level 3b at the end of their first semester and schedule a Junior Recital in their second semester, with permission from their instructor and the applied committee.

composition / music production juries

Composition and Music Production students have a separate jury procedure from vocalists and instrumentalists. At the end of every semester, students meet with the composition faculty to review their portfolio. The work done throughout the semester, and the overall contents of the portfolio, determine retention and advancement within the program. Detailed information about portfolio contents can be found at [portfolio guidelines: link TBD].

instrumental and vocal juries

Progress juries: A Progress Jury is performed during finals week in each semester that a student is not performing an Advancement or Recital Jury, generally at the end of the first semester of each level. Progress Juries assess whether the student is on track to meet the benchmarks required for advancement at the end of the following semester.

Advancement juries: An Advancement Jury is performed at the end of Level 1b to advance to Level 2, and at the end of Level 2b to advance to Level 3. Students in Levels 1a, 1b, or 2a may also take an Advancement Jury to Level 3a at the end of any semester. Advancement from Level 3 to Level 4 takes the form of a Junior Recital Jury.

Recital juries: Junior Recital Juries serve as the Advancement Jury at the conclusion of Level 3b. Senior Recital Juries serve as the program culmination at the conclusion of Level 4b. Recital Juries are performed approximately four (4) weeks before the recital date, before a faculty committee. The student is notified of the date and time after the recital is booked. Successful completion of the jury is required to confirm the recital date.

  • A print-ready copy of the recital program must be presented to each faculty jury member (3 copies total), and an electronic copy submitted to the MRC at the time of the jury. The jury will not sign the jury form unless the program is ready to print. Current program guidelines are on the MRC website.
  • All performers listed on the program must be present at the Recital Jury. If a non-CSUEB performer cannot attend, the student must notify the Applied Music Committee in advance. Permission to submit a recording of a discrete portion may be granted.
  • The student is notified of the jury outcome within one week. If the student fails the Recital Jury, the recital is immediately canceled and the student has two weeks to reschedule for the following semester. A rescheduled recital may not take place in the same semester as the originally scheduled one.
alternative to rescheduling

Students with a minimum of 3 Applied Study units completed at CSUEB who do not wish to reschedule may perform a progress jury at the end of the semester. Passing that jury concludes their applied music study requirements.

jury assessment and outcomes

Students are assessed on multiple benchmarks for each level, scored on a 0 to 4 scale. Proficiency for the current level is a score of 3.

Each jury results in a pass or a fail. The outcome depends on the jury type, and on a fail it depends on the previous jury. Jury outcomes do not necessarily correspond to the academic grade for the semester.

discontinuation

A discontinued student may not register for Applied Study in the future, but may work privately and perform another jury at the end of the next semester. Faculty may discontinue any student whose jury performance fails to demonstrate progress.

recital performances

All students registered for applied music study are expected to fulfill recital performance requirements each academic year. Instrumentalists and vocalists follow the level-based recital schedule below. Composition and Music Production students fulfill public performance requirements through their jury and portfolio process (see section 04). Failure to follow these guidelines results in a failing grade for Applied Music study for the semester in which the performance was scheduled, and the process must begin again.

types of recitals

Studio Recital: scheduled by the applied instructor and including other students from the same studio.

Noon Recital: a performance on the Tuesday/Thursday University Hour Noon Recital series, scheduled by the student. Requires instructor approval.

Junior and Senior Recitals: scheduled by the student in coordination with their applied instructor, serving as public verification of Level 3 (Junior) or Level 4 (Senior) competency. See below.

Ad Hoc Recital: a recital that does not qualify as any of the above, open to all students with approval from the MRC. Ad Hoc recitals do not count toward the recital requirement, including recitals scheduled as part of courses outside the student's applied music course.

junior recital

A Junior Recital must take place prior to the successful conclusion of 2 semesters of applied music study at Level 3 and should include a minimum of 25 minutes of music on the student's major instrument. These requirements must be met to progress to Level 4. Repertoire is chosen in consultation with the applied instructor.

senior recital

A Senior Recital must take place prior to the successful conclusion of 2 semesters of applied music study at Level 4 and should include a minimum of 30 and a maximum of 45 minutes of music on the student's major instrument. No recital should exceed 50 minutes including an intermission. Repertoire is chosen in consultation with the applied instructor.

recital scheduling

The Music Resource Center (MRC) coordinates the scheduling of all department concerts, recitals, and festivals, including all faculty and student recitals and major performance group concerts. Any student wishing to schedule a degree or ad hoc recital, or to perform on a University Hour recital, should contact the MRC to book a performance date after securing approval from their applied instructor. Recital dates should be scheduled several weeks in advance to ensure availability.

  • Students should consult with all performers, coaches, and accompanists prior to scheduling a recital date.
  • Junior, Senior, and Ad Hoc recitals may not be scheduled during the last week of regular classes of a semester or during final exam week, nor during the weekend between those two weeks. Departmental approval is required for recitals scheduled during academic breaks.

recital accompanists and collaborators

It is the student's responsibility to secure a pianist when required for juries, recitals, and other performances. Pianists are required for all voice student juries and recitals, and for the Advancement Juries and recitals of instrumental students when a selection requires accompaniment. Students are strongly encouraged to enroll in Instrumental Coaching or Vocal Coaching (MUS 281/481, MUS 282/482) to meet accompanying needs.

The Department will supply a pianist for voice student juries and lower-division Advancement Juries for instrumental students not enrolled in coaching. Students must request a pianist at least 30 days in advance by providing the jury date, time, location, and a legible PDF of the music to the MRC. Department-supplied pianists will have one 15-minute practice session with the student on the day of the jury. Requests within 30 days may not be honored. Accompaniments by piano majors must be approved by the Piano Area Coordinator.

performance attire and etiquette

Juries and recitals are formal performances. Unless your instructor specifies otherwise, dress in concert black: professional, all-black clothing comfortable enough to play or sing in, with quiet, closed-toe shoes. Some areas, jazz in particular, set their own conventions, so confirm expectations with your applied instructor before the performance.

Audiences are part of the performance. Arrive before the program begins, silence phones, and stay through to the end out of respect for the performers. Photography and audio or video recording during a performance are allowed only when the performer and the MRC have arranged it in advance, since the MRC produces the official recordings of degree recitals and many department events.

major performing ensembles and chamber ensembles

Undergraduate students enrolled in applied study must also be enrolled in at least one Major Performing Ensemble in which they perform on their principal instrument. Students are encouraged to also participate in Chamber Ensembles. Ensemble offerings and meeting times vary by semester; current information is available at the ensembles page. Auditions and placement take place during the first ensemble meeting of each semester. Students should register for the course and attend the first class. Contact ensemble directors with any questions.

major performing ensembles

East Bay Singers (MUS 351B): the primary ensemble for all voice students. Comprised of 32 voices of both music majors and non-majors, the East Bay Singers perform regularly on campus, in the community, and on annual tours. Repertoire spans 600 years of choral music from around the world.

East Bay Jazz Orchestra (MUS 355): the primary ensemble for all students studying Jazz Applied. The flagship big band, with an extensive touring history in Europe and Brazil, focused on representing the oral and aural traditions of jazz alongside contemporary and cutting-edge works. Students studying jazz guitar, jazz bass, jazz piano, or jazz percussion may substitute the Jazz Combo (MUS 368) if unable to earn a spot in the Jazz Orchestra at auditions.

East Bay Wind Symphony (MUS 353): the primary ensemble for all Wind, Brass, and Percussion students. A flagship large ensemble of majors and non-majors, recognized for innovative programming and superb musicianship. The Wind Symphony performs on campus, throughout the East Bay, and on tours across California and surrounding states.

Guitar, piano, organ, and composition/music production majors may elect any Major Performing Ensemble that is practical for them, in consultation with ensemble directors. Music Education students are strongly encouraged to participate in as many ensembles as possible during their time at CSUEB.

chamber ensembles

The following chamber ensembles are open to music majors and non-majors and count toward the chamber ensemble requirement (MUS 360–369). Placement is by audition at the first class meeting.

Rock Band (MUS 360): open to singers, guitarists, bassists, keyboardists, and drummers/percussionists. Covers performance, stage presence, sound gear, and rock composition across many genres.

Chamber Ensemble (MUS 361): focused on repertoire for small combinations of instruments. Students develop musical communication and interpretative skills through works from a variety of periods and styles.

Percussion Ensemble (MUS 363): a chamber experience for percussionists and students with some percussion background. Covers traditional and contemporary percussion literature with one on-campus performance per semester.

Chamber Orchestra (MUS 365): open to strings (violin, viola, cello, bass). Performs diverse repertoire including classical, film, video game, pop, and rock, with opportunities to collaborate with guest artists and participate in special projects.

Electronic Music Ensemble (MUS 366): comprised of undergraduate and graduate students who expand the boundaries of composition and performance through analog and digital technologies, everyday objects, live electronics, fixed media, and performance-installation.

Piano Ensemble (MUS 367): intensive study of piano duet and piano duo repertoire across diverse styles. Students rehearse weekly with partners, receive coaching, and present a public recital at the end of the semester. Satisfies the upper-division chamber ensemble requirement and may be repeated.

Jazz Combo (MUS 368): small group jazz performance emphasizing improvisation, interaction, and personal voice. Includes jazz standards and contemporary pieces, with opportunities to arrange, compose, and perform in workshops with guest artists. Register for MUS 355 Jazz Orchestra.

Vocal Repertory Ensemble (MUS 369A): chamber ensemble focusing on Art Song and repertoire for solo voice and small and large vocal combinations, with emphasis on peer review and collaborative assessment of performance practice.

East Bay Jazz Singers (MUS 369C): a 12-voice ensemble chosen by vocal audition, specializing in vocal jazz and a cappella arrangements. Performs on and off campus, on tours, and in festivals, often alongside jazz combos, the Jazz Orchestra, or guest artists.

Chamber Singers (MUS 389B): small vocal ensemble performing diverse choral repertoire from Renaissance polyphony to contemporary compositions, with emphasis on vocal blend, intonation, and expressive singing.

A Cappella Ensemble (MUS 369D): small vocal ensemble performing music from classical, jazz, pop, and world music genres without instrumental accompaniment. Develops aural skills, rhythmic precision, and stylistic versatility.

music education

The Music Education program at CSUEB prepares students to become successful, career-ready music teachers. Coursework in music education can count toward the B.A. in Music and provides a direct pathway to the California Single Subject Teaching Credential. Students gain hands-on experience through coursework in conducting, instructional techniques, world music, and technology, as well as observation and teaching hours in Bay Area classrooms. The result is a strong foundation for those seeking to teach music and lead programs in California's schools.

Completion of the Single Subject Matter Preparation Certificate Program in Music streamlines students into the California Single Subject Teaching Credential in Music, authorizing instruction in any area of music from kindergarten through high school. Graduates teach throughout California and beyond as band, orchestra, and choir directors, elementary general music teachers, and instructors of music theory, composition, technology, and classroom guitar or piano. Many also continue to perform, compose, and maintain private teaching studios alongside their school positions.

how to obtain the certificate

The Music Education Certificate may currently be obtained in three ways.

  1. While pursuing a Bachelor of Arts (B.A.) in Music at CSUEB. Students must be admitted into the B.A. in Music degree to start the certificate, and follow the B.A. in Music application requirements.
  2. While pursuing a Master of Arts (M.A.) in Music at CSUEB. Students must be admitted into the M.A. in Music degree to start the certificate, and follow the M.A. in Music application requirements.
  3. As a stand-alone graduate-level certificate, for applicants already in possession of a bachelor's degree. These applicants apply directly through CSUApply.

fast 4+1 b.a./m.a. program

The FAST 4+1 Bachelor of Arts / Master of Arts (BA/MA) Program in Music at CSUEB offers students an accelerated pathway to earn both degrees in five years. It is designed for dedicated music students who wish to deepen their knowledge, refine their skills, and expand their career opportunities. Building on the strengths of the undergraduate program, the blended program integrates rigorous academic training with advanced studies in music theory, history, performance, composition, and contemporary issues including social justice and ecological sustainability. Students declare an area of emphasis (Performance in Classical or Jazz, Choral Conducting, or Composition) and engage in specialized coursework, applied lessons, ensemble participation, and collaborative projects.

eligibility

  • Current enrollment in the Bachelor of Arts in Music program at CSU East Bay.
  • Completion of lower-division coursework, general education requirements, and any American Institutions courses prior to transition.
  • Minimum cumulative GPA of 3.0 in music courses.

application process

Students apply to the blended program by January 15th of their junior year (between semesters 5 and 6). The application includes:

  1. Statement of Purpose. A one- to two-page document outlining the student's objectives for pursuing the blended BA/MA program, discussing academic and musical goals, and explaining how the program aligns with long-term aspirations.
  2. Faculty Recommendations. Two letters of recommendation from Music Department faculty who can assess the student's readiness for graduate-level study based on academic performance and musical abilities.
  3. Evidence of Abilities, depending on area of emphasis:
    • Performance (Classical/Jazz): an audition or submission of recordings demonstrating proficiency in the chosen instrument or vocal performance.
    • Choral Conducting: a demonstration of conducting skills, such as a video recording of a rehearsal or performance.
    • Composition: submission of original compositions or portfolios showcasing compositional skills.

For more information, visit the blended B.A./M.A. page.

music minor

A music minor is a great option for a student who is passionate about music but chooses to major in another field. The program aims to broaden and deepen students' understanding of the art of music and offers a good deal of flexibility. The Music Minor consists of 21 units, split into three categories: fundamentals (6 units), performance skills (3 units), and music electives (12 units).

how to declare

Contact the Department of Music before beginning the process of declaring the Music Minor (MB 2569, 510-885-3135, music@csueastbay.edu). Undergraduate students who wish to add the Music Minor should use the Change of Major, Minor, or Concentration form available on the MyCompass forms website. The form is routed electronically to the appropriate department for approval and then to the Registrar's Office for processing. Students can view the status of their submission by selecting the Submitted Forms tab on the MyCompass forms website.

minor requirements (21 units)

Music Fundamentals (6 units). This sequence covers basic fundamentals of music reading, writing, and singing, along with knowledge of chord construction and basic music progressions. Students with basic prior knowledge of music may substitute these units for other pre-approved lower-division music units after a successful assessment from the Music Department.

Performance Skills (3 units). Placement is determined by the Music Department based on the student's skills and needs. Students may substitute no more than 1 applied unit (private lessons on an instrument or voice) with department permission. Students may substitute at most 2 performance skills units with units taken in a major performing ensemble and/or chamber ensemble.

Music Electives (12 units). Take 12 units of music electives in consultation with a departmental advisor, at least 3 of which must be in a performance ensemble. Students may substitute at most 2 elective units with applied units (private lessons on an instrument, voice, or composition/music production) with department permission. All students taking applied lessons are required to be registered for at least 5 music units, 1 of which must be a major performing ensemble.

academic policies

academic integrity

As a member of the academic community, you are expected to act with integrity and to avoid plagiarism, unauthorized collaboration, and other forms of cheating. By enrolling, you agree to uphold the University's standards of academic integrity. In music study this also means presenting your own work at juries and recitals, properly crediting arrangements, editions, and sources, and disclosing any use of generative AI in courses that permit it. The full policy is at csueastbay.edu/aps/academic-policies/academic-dishonesty.html.

grades and academic standing

Music majors and music minors must earn a grade of C– or better in a course for it to count toward major or minor requirements. Applied study and ensemble grades are set as described in the relevant syllabus, and for applied study they follow from the jury outcome (see section 04).

The University requires a minimum 2.0 cumulative GPA for good academic standing. A GPA below 2.0 places a student on academic probation and, if it is not resolved, can lead to disqualification. Grade symbols such as Incomplete (I) and Withdrawal (W), and the rules that govern them, are defined in the University Catalog. Check your standing and your options with your music advisor and the Registrar's Office.

accommodations

For disability and other learning-related needs and accommodations, including assistance during an emergency evacuation, communicate with your instructor as early as possible. Students with disabilities are also encouraged to contact Accessibility Services at csueastbay.edu/accessibility to meet with a counselor about their options, including rights under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act. Accommodations apply to lessons, juries, and recitals as much as to classroom courses. Arrange any performance-related accommodation with your instructor and the MRC in advance.

appeals and grievances

A student who wishes to contest a course grade should first discuss it with the instructor. If the matter is unresolved, grade appeals and academic grievances follow the University process described in the Catalog: Grade Appeals and Academic Grievances.

To contest a jury or recital outcome, submit a written request to the Department Chair within ten (10) business days of being notified, stating the grounds for the appeal. The Chair will convene available applied faculty to review the recording and the relevant materials, and will respond in writing. Where a jury or recital outcome determines a course grade, it may afterward be carried through the University grade-appeal process above.

health, safety, and student support

The CSU East Bay Department of Music, as required by the National Association of Schools of Music (NASM), is obligated to inform students, faculty, and staff of the health and safety issues, hazards, and procedures inherent in music practice, performance, teaching, and listening, both in general and as applicable to their specific specializations. This includes basic information regarding the maintenance of hearing, vocal, and musculoskeletal health and injury prevention. It also includes instruction on the use, proper handling, and operation of potentially dangerous materials, equipment, and technology as applicable to specific program offerings or experiences.

Although the music department gives the highest priority to health and safety throughout its policies, protocols, and operations, the primary factor in your health and safety is you, and depends largely on your personal decisions. You are personally responsible for avoiding risk and preventing injuries to yourself before, during, and after studying at CSU East Bay Department of Music. The policies and procedures developed and followed by the Department do not alter or cancel any individual's personal responsibility, or in any way shift personal responsibility for the results of any individual's personal decisions or actions to the university.

Please refer to the Health and Safety page on the Department's website for a list of resources on hearing, vocal, and musculoskeletal health, as well as numerous other resources related to the study of music.
