Course Information

Catalog description: The exploration and analysis of music styles and structural forms of the European art music of the 18th and 19th centuries. Topics include phrase and sentence structures; thematic organization; binary and ternary forms; variation and rondo forms; slow movement forms; and sonata forms.

MUS 312 applies the harmonic and contrapuntal knowledge of the lower-division sequence to the analysis of complete musical works. The first week consolidates chromatic harmony within an analytical framework before moving into phrase structure, the formal types of tonal music, and sonata form. The course integrates written analysis, online ear training preparation, and applied work at the keyboard. In place of a midterm, the course uses three group quiz checkpoints distributed across the semester; a cumulative final exam in Week 15 covers the full arc of the material.

Student Learning Outcomes

Upon successful completion of this course, students will be able to:

  1. 01Demonstrate understanding of the forms used in tonal music, including sentence, period, binary, ternary, rondo, and sonata forms.
  2. 02Analyze and evaluate complete tonal works in their technical, aesthetic, and historical contexts, supporting interpretive claims with evidence from the score.
  3. 03Apply the harmonic and contrapuntal knowledge of the lower-division sequence to extended musical works.
  4. 04Identify phrase structure, hypermeter, and formal boundaries both visually and aurally.
  5. 05Construct written analyses using appropriate terminology, Roman numerals, figured bass, and formal labels.
  6. 06Implement the principles and concepts studied in class via performance on the student's major instrument or voice.

Learning Benchmarks

By the end of the semester, students who have successfully engaged with the course material should be able to demonstrate the following:

Phrase Structure

Students can identify and describe sentences, periods, and phrase pairs in musical scores, distinguishing antecedent from consequent, parallel from contrasting, and locating cadential points with precision.

Formal Analysis

Students can analyze complete movements in binary, ternary, rondo, and sonata forms, identifying tonal and thematic processes and supporting their readings with evidence from the score.

Aural Skills

Students can sight-sing through modulations to closely related keys, identify modulation by ear, and hear hypermeter and formal boundaries in musical examples.

Making the time work

MUS 312 is a hybrid course: part of the class time functions as a seminar, where we read, discuss, and analyze together; part of it functions as an activity, where you will be at the desk or the keyboard, working through problems and refining analyses in real time with your classmates. Show up prepared, with the scores and readings in hand, and show up ready to engage.

The out-of-class time in this course is heavier than the lower-division theory sequence and it is structured around three kinds of work: reading and annotating scores, completing written analyses and workbook exercises, and working through the weekly online Auralia ear training assignments (due Sunday evenings during the first two-thirds of the semester). All three are cumulative, and all three reward daily attention over weekend cramming.

Use the practice rooms for sight-singing and keyboard work. See the Music Department facilities page for access and hours.

Stay organized. Analytical work in this course produces a growing archive of annotated scores, written analyses, and reading notes. A dedicated folder or binder is essential: keep scores and analyses in chronological order so you can return to them during revisions, and so the connections between works across the semester remain visible.

The students who do well in MUS 312 treat analysis as an iterative practice: a first reading produces observations, a second reading tests them, a third reading revises. Build that rhythm into your week.

Technology: Canvas for all course materials and grade tracking. MuseScore (free) is available for optional digital notation and analytical mark-up. Auralia is the delivery platform for the weekly ear training assignments; the instructor will provide an access code at the start of the semester.

Course Requirements

This course has four categories of graded work:

Written Assignments (35%): Weekly analytical work drawn from the Burstein and Straus workbook, supplementary exercises, and complete-movement analyses. These are the core of the course's written skill-building work, and they scale in length and complexity as the semester progresses.

Ear Training Assignments (25%): Weekly ear training work delivered online through Auralia, focused on chromatic melody, secondary triads, modulation identification, and short harmonic dictation. Assignments are due Sunday at 11:59pm during Weeks 2–11, front-loading the ear training work across the first two-thirds of the semester. A brief in-class check-in on Mondays supports the online work (reviewing common difficulties, performing selected examples live) without duplicating it; there are no separate in-person performance assessments.

Quizzes (20%): Group quiz checkpoints held at regular intervals across the semester, replacing a formal midterm. Students work in groups of two or three, troubleshooting the material together rather than individually. This is deliberate: the group format asks you to externalize your reasoning, catch each other's errors, and build the habit of talking through theory with peers, which is how analysis actually gets done in rehearsals, coachings, and score study. Quizzes test vocabulary, chord and form identification, and short analytical problems. Quizzes cannot be made up if missed without a documented excuse.

Final Exam (20%): A cumulative final exam held in class during Week 15, covering the full semester: formal analysis, harmonic vocabulary, and selected listening. Full details will be posted on Canvas ahead of the exam.

Course Policies

Attendance. Regular attendance is essential. Much of the work in MUS 312 happens in the room: analytical discussion, live ear training performance, and quiz checkpoints cannot be replicated outside of class. If you need to miss a class, please email the instructor in advance when possible. More than three unexcused absences will affect your grade.

Late work. Assignments submitted after the deadline will receive a 2% deduction per day late, up to a maximum deduction of 50%. This means submitting late is always better than not submitting, and work will never receive less than 50% credit solely due to lateness. The quality of the work is still evaluated normally. If you anticipate significant delays or challenges, please communicate early, in person or through Canvas.

Syllabus changes. Unanticipated circumstances may require adjustments to this syllabus. Any changes will be discussed with students before implementation and communicated in person and through Canvas.

Generative AI Use Policy

Generative AI tools (such as ChatGPT, Claude, DALL·E, and similar) may be used in this course with documentation and citation. AI can be useful for brainstorming or organizing ideas, but the listening, thinking, and writing must be your own. AI cannot replace listening or reflection, and substituting AI-generated content for required analysis violates course policy.
