Course Information

Catalog description: The first of three intensive courses that introduce students to the inner workings of European art music of the 18th and 19th centuries. This course covers diatonic harmony, as well as part-writing, voice-leading, and harmonization techniques.

This course assumes completion of MUS 108 or equivalent preparation. Students enter with a working knowledge of notation, scales, intervals, and triads, and will develop those fundamentals into a full working vocabulary of tonal harmony. The course is writing- and analysis-intensive: expect to spend significant time at the desk and at the keyboard.

Student Learning Outcomes

Upon successful completion of this course, students will be able to:

  1. 01Read, write, and analyze the fundamental materials of tonal music using correct terminology and nomenclature.
  2. 02Construct and identify all triad and seventh chord types in root position and inversion.
  3. 03Write four-part (SATB) harmonic progressions using I, V, V⁷, and their inversions, applying basic voice-leading principles.
  4. 04Compose and analyze two-voice counterpoint through fourth species.
  5. 05Apply theoretical concepts at the keyboard and in performance on the student's primary instrument or voice.

Learning Benchmarks

By the end of the semester, students who have successfully engaged with the course material should be able to demonstrate the following:

Theory I

Students can write and identify all triad and seventh chord types, construct SATB progressions using I, V, V7, I6, and V6, apply voice leading principles, write simple two-voice counterpoint through fourth species, and use all inversions of the V7 chord correctly.

Making the time work

This course is classified as an activity course for a reason. Class time is not a lecture you can passively receive; it is structured around writing, analyzing, and working through problems in the room with your classmates. You will be at the desk or the keyboard most days, and much of what you take away from a given class comes from the work you do during it. Show up prepared, and show up ready to engage.

More importantly, you are taking three courses concurrently (Theory, Aural Skills, and Keyboard), and the combined out-of-class load across the three is substantial. Plan for it.

One schedule that works well is roughly two hours a day, Monday through Thursday, split between the three courses. Daily short sessions will outperform occasional long ones in every one of these courses. Use the practice rooms! See the Music Department facilities page for access and hours.

Concise Introduction to Tonal Harmony Workbook, 3rd edition
Burstein and Straus, W. W. Norton & Company
Softcover ISBN: 978-1-324-08808-0

Must be purchased alongside the textbook. Workbook exercises are a required component of lesson assignments.

Other materials: Manuscript paper and pencils with erasers for all written work.

Stay organized. This course generates a lot of paper. You will receive handouts, in-class exercises, and graded assignments almost every week, and the material is strictly cumulative: what you learn in week 3 is still in play in week 12. A folder or binder dedicated to this course is not optional in spirit, even if it is in practice.

A few habits that help: keep handouts in chronological order, annotate exercises when they are returned (your errors are a study guide), and review your notes before each class rather than only before exams. Part-writing is a skill, and skills erode without maintenance. The students who do well in this course are not necessarily the most naturally talented. They are the ones who keep their materials organized and return to them regularly.

Technology: Canvas for all course materials and grade tracking. MuseScore (free) is available for optional digital notation work. Auralia ear training software is used in the co-requisite MUS 115; the instructor will provide an access code at the start of the semester.

Course Requirements

This course has four categories of graded work:

Lesson Exercises (40%): Weekly written assignments from the Burstein and Straus workbook and supplementary materials, graded for completion, accuracy, and application of voice-leading principles. These are the core of the course's skill-building work.

Quizzes (25%): Short in-class group quizzes testing vocabulary, chord identification, and part-writing skills. Students work in groups of two or three, troubleshooting the material together rather than individually. This is deliberate: the group format asks you to externalize your reasoning, catch each other's errors, and build the habit of talking through theory with peers, which is how theory actually gets used in rehearsals, coachings, and score study. Quizzes are cumulative in nature and cannot be made up if missed without a documented excuse.

Midterm Exam (15%): A written exam covering the first half of the course (Chapters 1–7), including chord construction, SATB part-writing, and analysis. Held during Week 8.

Final Exam (20%): A cumulative written exam covering all material from the semester. Held during Week 15.

Course Policies

Attendance. Regular attendance is essential. Theory is learned incrementally and each class builds directly on the last. If you need to miss a class, please email the instructor in advance when possible. More than three unexcused absences will affect your grade.

Late work. Assignments submitted after the deadline will receive a 2% deduction per day late, up to a maximum deduction of 50%. This means submitting late is always better than not submitting, and work will never receive less than 50% credit solely due to lateness. The quality of the work is still evaluated normally. If you anticipate significant delays or challenges, please communicate early, in person or through Canvas.

Syllabus changes. Unanticipated circumstances may require adjustments to this syllabus. Any changes will be discussed with students before implementation and communicated in person and through Canvas.

Generative AI Use Policy

Generative AI tools (such as ChatGPT, Claude, DALL·E, and similar) may be used in this course with documentation and citation. AI can be useful for brainstorming or organizing ideas, but the listening, thinking, and writing must be your own. AI cannot replace listening or reflection, and substituting AI-generated content for required analysis violates course policy.
