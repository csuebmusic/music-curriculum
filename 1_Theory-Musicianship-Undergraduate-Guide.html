About This Document

This guide maps the lower division core sequence for the B.A. in Music, coordinating content, pacing, and benchmarks across the three concurrent tracks: Music Theory, Aural Skills, and Keyboard Musicianship. It is intended as a reference for instructors teaching within this sequence, to support cross-track coordination and consistent expectations.

Concepts introduced in theory should be reinforced aurally in the same week or the week following, and keyboard work should connect to both wherever possible.

Sequence at a Glance

All three tracks run concurrently within each semester. MUS 108 is a prerequisite for the core sequence and is offered in both Fall and Spring; music majors typically take it in Fall of Year 1. Form and Analysis (MUS 312) and 20th and 21st Century Styles and Techniques (MUS 410) are upper-division courses that draw on the full lower-division sequence.

Course Classifications and Expected Workload

Course classifications follow the CSU Course Classification System. The classification determines both contact hours per unit and maximum class size. Under the CSU credit hour definition, each unit of credit represents one hour of in-class instruction and a minimum of two hours of out-of-class student work per week across a 15-week semester. C10 (music activity, small group) courses carry two contact hours per unit rather than one, reflecting the activity-based nature of the instruction. All hours below are per week during the semester.

End-of-Semester Benchmarks — MUS 108

Reading & Rhythm

Students can read pitches in treble and bass clef, interpret note values and rests in simple and compound meters, and correctly identify and write ties, dotted rhythms, syncopation, triplets, and duplets.

Scales & Keys

Students can construct major scales and all three forms of minor scales, identify key signatures through the full circle of fifths, and identify and construct intervals by number and quality. Roman numeral analysis and form are not covered.

Harmony

Students can construct and identify major, minor, diminished, and augmented triads in root position. Triad inversions, seventh chords, Roman numerals, and formal analysis are reserved for the Theory I sequence.

End-of-Semester Benchmarks — Spring Year 1

Theory I

Students can write and identify all triad and seventh chord types, construct SATB progressions using I, V, V7, I6, and V6, apply voice leading principles, write simple two-voice counterpoint through fourth species, and use all inversions of the V7 chord correctly.

Aural Skills I

Students can sight-sing diatonic melodies in major and minor keys using solfège or scale degrees, take melodic dictation in simple and compound meters including quadruple beat division, identify intervals by number and quality, and perform chromatic neighbor tones. Minor mode is introduced and consolidated within this semester.

Keyboard I

Students can play major and minor five-finger patterns and their triads in all common keys, perform all four triad qualities (major, minor, augmented, diminished), play major scales in tetrachord position with correct fingering, and use the damper pedal. Basic sight-reading and harmonization skills are established.

MUS 112 / 116 / 119 — Theory II, Aural Skills II, Keyboard II

Fall — Year 2

Second semester of the concurrent core sequence. Theory II moves through diatonic harmony in depth, Aural Skills II consolidates minor mode and introduces harmonic listening, and Keyboard II builds full harmonic vocabulary at the keyboard. All three tracks converge on functional harmony (dominant, subdominant, and primary chords in both major and minor) in the second half of the semester.

End-of-Semester Benchmarks — Fall Year 2

Theory II

Students can write and analyze SATB progressions using all diatonic triads and seventh chords, including vii°7, the cadential 6/4, sequences, and embellishing chords. They understand VI, III, and VII in functional context and can identify and apply other 6/4 chord types.

Aural Skills II

Students can sight-sing in major and minor modes with chromatic neighbors and dominant seventh chord skips, take melodic and harmonic dictation including bass lines and two-voice textures, perform syncopation and sextuple compound meter, and identify all four triad qualities by ear. Harmonic listening through basic chord progressions is introduced and consolidated.

Keyboard II

Students can play major and minor scales and arpeggios across Group 1 and 2 keys, perform primary chords in major and minor with block and broken patterns, harmonize short melodies using diatonic chords, sing a melody while playing a two-hand accompaniment, and realize simple lead-sheet symbols. Sight-reading in grand staff hands together is established.

End-of-Semester Benchmarks — Spring Year 2

Theory III

Students can write and analyze chromatic harmony including applied dominants, modal mixture, the Neapolitan sixth, augmented sixth chords, and altered chords. They can identify and write modulations to the dominant and closely related keys, and analyze chromatic sequences and chromatic modulation.

Aural Skills III

Students can identify all diatonic triads by ear, sight-sing melodies involving secondary triads and seventh chords, take dictation including chromatic passing tones and applied chord skips, and perform six-four figures and chromatic prefix neighbors in melodic context.

Keyboard III

Students can play major and minor scales and arpeggios in all key groups, perform secondary triads (ii, iii, vi) and seventh chords in harmonic context, play chromatic, whole-tone, and blues scales, and perform melodies and accompaniments in all six church modes. Alfred's Book 1 is complete.

MUS 312 — Form and Analysis

Fall — Year 3

Upper-division. Prerequisite: completion of the Theory I–III / Aural Skills I–III sequence. This course applies the harmonic and contrapuntal knowledge of the lower-division sequence to the analysis of complete musical works. The first week consolidates chromatic harmony within an analytical framework before moving into phrase structure, formal types, and sonata form. In place of a midterm, the course uses three group quiz checkpoints distributed across the semester, with a cumulative final exam in Week 15.

End-of-Semester Benchmarks — MUS 312

Phrase Structure

Students can identify and describe sentences, periods, and phrase pairs in musical scores, distinguishing antecedent from consequent, parallel from contrasting, and locating cadential points with precision.

Formal Analysis

Students can analyze complete movements in binary, ternary, rondo, and sonata forms, identifying tonal and thematic processes and supporting their readings with evidence from the score.

Aural Skills

Students can sight-sing through modulations to closely related keys, identify modulation by ear, and hear hypermeter and formal boundaries in musical examples.

MUS 410 — 20th and 21st Century Styles and Techniques

Spring — Year 3

Upper-division. Prerequisite: MUS 312. This course moves beyond tonal harmony into the analytical and compositional techniques of the 20th and 21st centuries: post-tonal pitch organization, modes, and advanced metric concepts. Karpinski chapters skipped in the lower-division sequence are integrated here alongside the Straus post-tonal content. Assessment is through quiz checkpoints and analytical or compositional projects.

End-of-Semester Benchmarks — MUS 410

Pitch Organization

Students can identify and analyze post-tonal pitch collections including pentatonic, octatonic, whole-tone, and diatonic sets, construct and manipulate pitch-class sets in normal and prime form, and analyze twelve-tone rows and their transformations.

Form and Style

Students can analyze formal processes in post-tonal and 21st-century works, identify centricity and referential collections, and situate works within the major compositional movements of the 20th and 21st centuries including serialism, spectralism, and minimalism.

Aural Skills

Students can sight-sing pentatonic melodies, perform in all church modes using both relative and parallel approaches, perform and notate advanced rhythmic patterns and metric complexities, and identify non-diatonic pitch collections and fragments of tonality by ear.
